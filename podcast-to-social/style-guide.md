# Content Style Guide — Podcast-to-Social Posts
### Seneca Chat · Content Factory

This guide governs every post generated by the podcast-to-social automation.
It is sourced directly from the Seneca Chat foundational documents:
Avatar Sheet, Offer Brief, Necessary Beliefs, and Research Doc.

---

## 1. The Audience — "The Overthinking High-Achiever"

**Who they are**
Smart, ambitious, 28–38. Already consuming Stoicism, philosophy, and self-improvement
content (books, podcasts, YouTube). They're not beginners. They're looking to *apply*
what they already know, not be introduced to it.

**Professional backgrounds**
Tech, consulting, finance, sales, founders, healthcare, lawyers, grad students, creators.

**Income**
Middle to upper-middle (~$3,000–$12,000+ / month). Willing to pay for tools that work.

**How they self-identify**
- "I'm disciplined… except when my mind hijacks me."
- "I want principles, not motivation."
- "Self-improvement is part of who I am."
- "I'm the calm one… until I'm not."

**What they're struggling with**
- Overthinking and anxiety loops — mental replay of conversations and decisions
- Emotional reactivity under pressure — snapping, regretting texts and emails
- Can't stick with habits — downloads apps, uses them 3 days, forgets
- Feeling "behind" mentally even when life looks fine externally

**What they want (short-term)**
- Get calm and clear in 2–5 minutes when spiraling
- Make hard decisions with less second-guessing
- A simple daily practice that doesn't feel like homework

**What they want (long-term)**
- Become "harder to rattle" — emotional resilience
- Become more disciplined and consistent (identity shift)
- Live with more meaning and self-respect

**What they hate**
- Fluffy, vague, overly spiritual self-help
- Motivational quotes with no practical application
- Generic AI that sounds like a chatbot
- Content that talks down to them or assumes they're a beginner
- Overhyped language ("unlock your potential", "life-changing", "incredible")

## 2. Voice

Write like a sharp friend who reads Marcus Aurelius and actually applies it.
Not a content creator. Not a wellness brand. Not a therapist.

**Principles:**
- **Direct.** No filler. Cut every word that doesn't earn its place.
- **Framework-first.** Principles and models over vibes and feelings.
- **Honest.** State things plainly. Don't hedge every sentence.
- **Concrete.** Specific > abstract. Example > generality.
- **Confident without moralising.** State the idea and let the reader decide.
- **Earned complexity.** Short sentences for punchy ideas. Longer sentences only
  when the idea genuinely needs it.

---

## 3. What to Write (and What Not to Write)

### Write this
- Observations the reader will recognise as true the moment they read them
- Counterintuitive framings: "Most people X. The Stoics said Y."
- Principle + application: "The Stoics called this *amor fati*. In practice, it means..."
- Honest tension: the gap between knowing what to do and being able to access it
- Identity language (who they want to *become*, not just what they want to *feel*)

### Never write this
- "Unlock your potential"
- "Life-changing" / "game-changer" / "powerful" / "incredible"
- "In this episode, [guest] shares..."
- "You need to hear this"
- Anything that could appear on a motivational poster
- Medical or therapeutic claims: "reduces anxiety", "treats stress", "heals"
- "This app..." (in the main body — the CTA handles the app reference)

## 4. The Big Idea (for context)

Seneca Chat's core positioning:
> "A wise voice on demand — so you respond with clarity instead of reacting on autopilot."

This is **philosophy as an operating system for modern life**, delivered in the moment
you need it. Not "learn philosophy." Not "read quotes." Use it *right now*.

The mechanism: framework-grounded AI guidance + a low-friction daily practice
(quotes + rituals + reflections) that compounds into identity-level calm and discipline.

These posts exist to reach people who are already interested in this kind of thinking.
The content itself *is* the product demonstration.

## 5. Post Structure by Platform

### X (Twitter) — Thread Format

| Tweet | Purpose | Length |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hook — sharpest idea from the episode. Must make someone stop scrolling. | ≤ 240 chars |
| 2 | First key insight — practical, principle-based | ≤ 270 chars |
| 3 | Second insight or best quote from the episode | ≤ 270 chars |
| 4 (optional) | Third insight — only if genuinely warranted | ≤ 270 chars |
| Final | One-line CTA + episode link | ≤ 270 chars |

**Hook guidelines:**
- Never start with the podcast name or "In this episode..."
- Lead with the idea, the conflict, or the question
- Tension and contrast work: "Most people do X. The Stoics said Y."
- An open loop works: "The reason smart people get stuck isn't what you think."
- A stark observation works: "You can know exactly what to do and still not be able to do it."

**No hashtags. No emojis by default. No thread numbering.**

---

### Reddit — Text Post Format

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Title | The core insight or sharpest question — not the episode title |
| Hook (2–3 sentences) | What the episode is about and why it matters |
| Key Takeaways (3–5 bullets) | Substantive, actionable, not padded |
| Notable Quote (optional) | One standout line, clearly from the transcript |
| Source line | Episode title + channel + link |
| Closing question (recommended) | Drives comments and engagement |
| CTA (optional) | Last line only, only if it fits naturally — never forced |

**Reddit-specific rules:**
- Add genuine analysis or framing — don't just summarise
- The post must provide value even without clicking the link
- No hype. No exclamation marks. No "must-listen."
- The CTA (if used) reads as a casual aside, not a pitch

## 6. The Call to Action

The CTA is **one line, at the very end, never in the body of the post.**

Its job is not to sell — it's to create a breadcrumb for people who are already interested.

**The right tone (examples):**
- "If applying philosophy to real moments interests you → link in my profile."
- "More on this kind of thinking → link in profile."
- "I've been building something around this exact idea if anyone's curious → link in profile."
- "If this framing is useful, there's more where it came from → link in profile."

**Rules:**
- Never name the app in the post body
- Never say "download", "subscribe", "try", "sign up", or "check out"
- Never use exclamation marks in the CTA
- Never put the CTA anywhere except the last line
- If the episode topic doesn't connect naturally to the app's use case: omit the CTA entirely

**What the profile link leads to:** the Seneca Chat App Store page.
The goal is traffic from people who are already resonating with this kind of content.

## 7. Necessary Beliefs (what the content must build over time)

These posts collectively serve to build the following beliefs in the audience:

1. My overthinking / reactivity / indecision is a real problem — and it's costing me.
2. Philosophy (especially Stoicism) is *practical* — something I can use in real moments.
3. When emotions spike, I can't reliably access my wise self — so I need fast, principle-based
   clarity in the moment.
4. An AI grounded in classic philosophers can give me non-generic guidance — frameworks,
   not fluffy motivation.
5. Tiny daily reps can compound into real resilience — and the right tool is simple enough
   that I'll actually stick with it.
6. The trial/subscription is transparent and worth it — this is a daily tool, not content.

Posts don't need to explicitly state any of these. They build them by being exactly
the kind of content the right person finds useful.

## 8. Sensitive Topics — Hard Rules

- **No medical or therapeutic claims.** Position as: practical guidance, mental fitness,
  philosophy-based coaching, reflection. Not therapy. Not treatment.
- **No anxiety/depression framing.** The product is for people who are functioning well
  and want to function better — not people in crisis.
- **No guarantees.** Don't promise outcomes ("you will feel calmer", "this will change...").
  Describe the mechanism and let the reader draw conclusions.
- **Privacy.** Never reference personal data, user conversations, or anything that implies
  the app stores sensitive information.
